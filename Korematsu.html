This controversial Supreme Court case upheld the internment of Japanese-Americans during World War II, ruling that national security outweighed individual rights—a decision now regarded as a grave injustice.

Facts

Following Pearl Harbor, Executive Order 9066 forced Japanese-Americans into relocation camps. Fred Korematsu refused to leave his home, was arrested, and appealed his conviction up to the Supreme Court.

Arguments

The government argued the relocation was necessary to prevent espionage and protect national security. Korematsu claimed the order violated his Fifth Amendment rights and deprived him of liberty without due process.

Judgement

In a 6–3 decision, the Supreme Court upheld the exclusion order, ruling it was justified by wartime necessity, not racial prejudice. Dissenting justices condemned it as unconstitutional and racist.

Impact

Korematsu drew heavy criticism as a “legalization of racism.” It was later repudiated in 2018, with Chief Justice Roberts declaring it “gravely wrong.” The case remains a civil liberties warning.

Quick Facts

Year: 1944

Court: U.S. Supreme Court

Legal Area: 5th Amendment

Key Principle: military necessity and national security

Outcome: Govenrment won, deemed curfews were justified for wartime necessity

R v Dudley and Stephens is a landmark criminal law case that established necessity is no defense to murder, shaping moral responsibility and legal principles in life-and-death situations worldwide.

Facts

After a shipwreck, four sailors were stranded without food. Dudley and Stephens killed the weakest member, Parker, for survival. They were later rescued and charged with his murder.

Arguments

Dudley and Stephens claimed necessity, arguing killing Parker was vital to survive. The prosecution argued it was unjustified, deliberate, and dangerous to allow necessity as a defense for murder.

Judgement

The court ruled Dudley and Stephens guilty, holding that necessity cannot justify murder. Their death sentence was commuted to six monthsâ€™ imprisonment due to the extreme survival circumstances.

Impact

The case became a cornerstone of criminal law, confirming that necessity cannot justify murder. It sparked lasting debate on morality versus legality and remains a key precedent in ethical and legal discussions.

Quick Facts

Year: 1884

Court: High Court of Justice

Legal Area: Criminal (Murder)

Key Principle: Necessity

Outcome: Dudley and Stephens found guilty, established necessity is not a valid defense for murder

Entick v Carrington is a landmark case in constitutional law that established the limits of executive power and reinforced the right to privacy and property — principles that continue to shape modern civil liberties.

Facts

Government officials, acting under a warrant from the Earl of Halifax, searched John Entick’s home for allegedly seditious writings. They broke locks and seized papers without consent, prompting Entick to sue for trespass and damages.

Arguments

Carrington argued that the search was lawful because it was conducted under a valid government warrant. Entick contended the warrant had no legal basis and that his home was unlawfully entered and damaged.

Judgement

Lord Camden ruled in favor of Entick, declaring the warrant invalid as it lacked legal authority. He affirmed that government officials cannot infringe on private property without explicit legal justification.

Impact

The ruling became a cornerstone of constitutional law, defining limits on state power and protecting individual rights. It influenced the U.S. Fourth Amendment and remains a foundational precedent for privacy and liberty.

Quick Facts

Year: 1765

Court: King's Bench

Legal Area: Civil Liberties

Key Principle: Rule of Law

Outcome: Entick won; established state agents can't search private property without a lawful basis.

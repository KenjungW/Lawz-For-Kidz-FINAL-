    Dietrich v. The Queen is a landmark decision in the High Court of Australia. This decision made it so a person who is accused of serious charges are entitled to legal representation.

    Facts

    Olaf Deitrich was charged for smuggling heroin into Australia. He was refused public funded legal representation and was found guilty and sentenced to 7 years behind bars. Dietrich then appealed to the Court of Criminal Appeal, who refused to hear his appeal.

    Arguments

    Dietrich argued that a person must be entitled to a legal assistance without payment in a case where one doesn't have the means to pay. The prosecution argued that there is no constitutional or common law that states the right to legal representation at public expense, and delaying courts would reduce efficiency.

    Judgement

    In a 5-2 decision, the High Court ruled that a person convicted of a serious crime must be provided legal representation, otherwise adjournment would be granted and the trial be delayed. The dissenting justices argued that a fair trial did not include publically funded representation.

    Impact

    Human rights lawyers praised the decision for emphasizing the right to a fair trial. After the ruling, an Australian Senate commission recieved submissions that state the ruling could result in legal aid being redirected from family and civil law to criminal law.

    Quick Facts

    Year: 1992

    Court: High Court of Australia

    Legal Area: Criminal Procedure

    Key Principle: Right to Council

    Outcome: Dietrich won, a person charged with a violent crime is entitled to legal council

This landmark case established the “clear and present danger” test, ruling that speech encouraging resistance to the draft during wartime was not protected under the First Amendment.

Facts

Charles Schneck distributed leaflets urging resistance to the WWI draft, claiming it violated the 13th Amendment. He was arrested under the Espionage Act for interfering with the U.S. war effort.

Arguments

The U.S. argued the leaflets posed a “clear and present danger” to national security. Schneck claimed his speech was protected under the First Amendment and the Espionage Act suppressed freedoms.

Judgement

The Supreme Court unanimously ruled for the U.S., holding that speech creating a clear danger—like falsely shouting “fire” in a theater—is not protected under the First Amendment.

Impact

The case became a key precedent in defining speech limits. Critics argued it restricted civil liberties, later influencing broader protections for free speech as interpretation of the First Amendment evolved.

Quick Facts

Year: 1919

Court: U.S. Supreme Court

Legal Area: 1st Amendment

Key Principle: National Security

Outcome: Government won, established limits to free speech

Known as the “snail in the bottle” case, Donoghue v Stevenson is one of the most important legal cases in modern history. It established the principle of negligence and the duty of care owed to others — concepts that still underpin consumer protection laws worldwide today.

Facts

Mrs. Donoghue drank ginger beer purchased by a friend in a café in Paisley, Scotland. The opaque bottle contained a decomposed snail, causing her shock and illness. She sued the manufacturer, Mr. Stevenson, despite having no contract with him.

Arguments

Stevenson argued no duty of care existed without a contract or dangerous product. Donoghue claimed negligence caused her harm and manufacturers should owe a duty of care based on circumstances, not contracts.

Judgement

The House of Lords ruled in favor of Donoghue, establishing that manufacturers owe a duty of care to the ultimate consumers of their products. Lord Atkin’s famous “neighbor principle” became the foundation for the modern law of negligence.

Impact

The case revolutionized tort law and remains a key precedent taught in law schools globally. It laid the groundwork for consumer rights, personal injury claims, and public safety standards worldwide.

Quick Facts

Year: 1932

Court: House of Lords

Legal Area: Tort (Negligence)

Key Principle: Duty of Care

Outcome: Donoghue won; established liability without contract

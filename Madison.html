This landmark case established the principle of judicial review, empowering the Supreme Court to strike down laws deemed unconstitutional — cementing the Constitution as the nation’s highest authority.

Facts

After President Thomas Jefferson ordered James Madison to withhold William Marbury’s judicial commission signed by outgoing President John Adams, Marbury invoked the Judiciary Act to petition the Supreme Court for a writ of mandamus, demanding the delivery of his rightful appointment.

Arguments

Madison argued the commission was invalid and the Judiciary Act unconstitutional. Marbury contended his commission was legally binding and that the Act gave him the right to court enforcement.

Judgement

The Supreme Court ruled Marbury was entitled to his commission but found the Judiciary Act unconstitutional, establishing judicial review and affirming the Constitution’s supremacy over all other laws.

Impact

Initially debated, Marbury v. Madison became the foundation of American constitutional law, empowering courts to nullify unconstitutional acts and permanently defining the separation of powers in U.S. governance.

Quick Facts

Year: 1803

Court: U.S. Supreme Court

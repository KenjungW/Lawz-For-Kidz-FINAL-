This Supreme Court case affirmed that the Second Amendment protects an individual’s right to own firearms for lawful purposes, such as self-defense, marking a pivotal moment for gun rights and constitutional interpretation.

Facts

The District of Columbia’s 1975 Firearms Control Act banned private handgun ownership except for law enforcement. Security officer Dick Heller, who wanted a handgun for home protection, was denied a permit and sued, arguing the ban violated his 2nd Amendment rights.

Arguments

The District of Columbia argued the Second Amendment applied only to militias and that restricting handguns protected public safety. Heller countered that the ban violated his individual right to possess firearms for lawful purposes, including self-defense.

Judgement

In a 5–4 decision, the Supreme Court ruled in favor of Heller, holding that the Second Amendment protects an individual’s right to possess firearms for lawful purposes, such as self-defense in the home. The D.C. handgun ban was declared unconstitutional.

Impact

The ruling was praised by gun rights advocates and criticized by those supporting gun control. Legal scholars noted it redefined the Second Amendment as protecting individual rather than collective rights and significantly influenced later gun legislation.

Quick Facts

Year: 2008

Court: U.S. Supreme Court
